    Compliance & Risk — Overview

    The Compliance & Risk screen evaluates every in-scope device against four control dimensions: MDM enrolment, encryption, patch currency, and recent activity. Each device is placed in the highest-severity bucket it qualifies for, and the headline score is the share of devices that are fully Compliant against the board target.

    Risk segmentation stratifies the estate into four bands, applied in order of severity: Critical — unmanaged (no MDM enrolment, full shadow IT exposure), High — not encrypted (managed but no BitLocker or FileVault — PDPA breach risk if lost), Medium — patch overdue (managed and encrypted but missing a current patch baseline), and Low — compliant (no open issues). A device that hits multiple bands counts only in the most severe.

    The three insight cards translate the segmentation into prioritised remediation. Card 1 — Windows 10 patch debt isolates Win10 devices that are patch-overdue, the typical cyber-insurance trigger. Card 2 — Encryption gap counts MDM-enrolled devices without disk encryption, the PDPA exposure. Card 3 — Quick win models the percentage-point uplift to the compliance score if patch debt alone were resolved, and indicates whether that closes the gap to the board target. Each card uses three-state logic — not applicable when the underlying population is empty under the current filter, clean when the population exists with zero issues, or warn / crit by severity when issues are present.

    Utilisation & Optimisation — Overview

    The Utilisation & Optimisation screen analyses every in-scope device against three optimisation levers: Redeploy, Overspecified, and Refresh deferral. Each lever combines utilisation telemetry, model class, age and battery health into a per-device classification that drives the headline annual savings figure.

    Redeploy candidates are devices with utilisation status of Underused or Idle — active fewer than ~4 hours per day but otherwise in good health. These are prime for reallocation to new starters instead of new procurement.

    Overspecified assets are high-spec devices (top model class) deployed in roles where utilisation is not Active. Right-sizing on the next refresh recovers the per-unit price premium.

    Refresh deferral candidates are devices flagged for refresh that remain healthy: battery ≥ 50%, age < 60 months, and well-utilised. They can safely slip the refresh window by 12 months, deferring capex.

    Acceptable Use Compliance — Overview

    All figures on the screen — the headline compliance score, the four status pills, the risk category bars, the action cards, the department heat map, the acknowledgement pipeline, and the flagged-devices table — are computed live from the in-scope dataset.     Power & Carbon Analytics — Overview

    The Power & Carbon screen models annual energy use, cost, and CO₂ emissions for every in-scope device. Each device is assigned a power profile by platform and model class (idle, typical, peak, and effective wattage) which yields an annual kWh figure. Cost is then derived as annual kWh × the active tenant's electricity tariff (e.g. MYR 0.509/kWh on TNB Tariff B), and CO₂ uses the local grid carbon factor (0.233 kg CO₂/kWh on the Malaysia grid). Headline totals are scaled from the sample dataset up to the full estate.

    The five hero metrics show estate-level annual kWh, annual CO₂ in kilograms, annual energy spend, average kWh per device, and the annual saving opportunity. Saving opportunity is the counterfactual cost difference between the current Windows fleet and an equivalent fleet of MacBook Air M2 devices — Apple silicon's much lower idle and typical wattage make this the largest single energy lever available. The full calculation panel underneath the hero tiles shows every line of the working so the customer can audit it.

    The three insight cards highlight the largest contributors and quickest wins: the platform with the highest per-device draw, the carbon impact of refresh waves shifting Windows → Apple silicon, and idle-state waste. The idle waste panel models always-on devices left powered but unused for ~23 hours/day; enforcing a sleep-after-15-minutes policy on the Windows fleet via Intune typically recovers ~30% of idle draw with zero hardware change. macOS already benefits from aggressive default power management via Jamf, so the policy lift is Windows-side.

    Endpoint Economics — Overview

    The Endpoint Economics screen is the commercial view of the estate. It composes a per-device total cost of ownership (TCO) from four inputs: hardware amortisation (capex ÷ refresh cycle), support cost per platform, energy cost (carried over from Power & Carbon), and a compliance risk surcharge that uplifts the support cost on non-compliant devices. The per-platform constants live in tenant config so they can be tuned per customer.

    The five hero KPIs show average annual TCO per device, the total compliance cost exposure (the monetised value of the breach risk if open issues remain unresolved), the total annual saving opportunity available to the customer, the projected 3-year CO₂ reduction if the estate shifts toward Apple silicon, and the net ROI on the EIS subscription itself. Net ROI ties out exactly to the Dashboard, Utilisation, and Reporting Snapshot screens — they all draw from a single shared savings calculator.

    The compliance risk-to-financial bridge translates open issues into MYR exposure across five categories — PDPA breach risk on non-compliant devices, patch debt, shadow IT (unmanaged endpoints), encryption gaps, and high-risk AUP devices — each multiplied by a per-device cost. The savings opportunity stack decomposes the headline savings figure into its four contributing levers — redeployment of underused devices, healthy refresh deferral, right-sizing of overspecified hardware, and the Windows → MacBook Air M2 energy switch — each independently sized, independently actionable, and recoverable within the first 12 months. The 3-year carbon roadmap projects the CO₂ reduction trajectory as Windows refresh waves transition to lower-wattage Apple silicon. Together these three views give the CFO conversation a defensible, line-by-line basis.

    The acquisition-model toggle below lets the CFO flip the same estate between three lenses: cash purchase (today's default, where lifecycle cost is dispersed across multiple budget lines), mixed current state, and CHN DaaS reconstruction (10–15% below retail with bundled AppleCare/warranty, 24/7 support, and end-of-life disposal absorbed into the monthly fee). The Estate Reconstruction Opportunity KPI shows the annual run-rate gap between the current acquisition model and a fully reconstructed DaaS estate — typically several times larger than the in-year optimisation savings.

    Digital Employee Experience — Overview

    The DEX screen measures how well every endpoint actually performs the work the employee is paid to do. It combines device telemetry from Windows Performance Analyzer (WPA) (boot duration, login time, CPU/RAM/disk pressure, crashes, network quality) with application performance for the line-of-business apps the user runs every day — Microsoft 365, ERP suites (SAP, Oracle, Workday, Salesforce), and industry-specific tooling.

    Identity is anchored on Active Directory as the master device identifier — every record carries the AD object GUID and User Principal Name as the join keys against which Intune (Windows), Jamf (Apple) and WPA telemetry are reconciled. AD is the single system of record; MDM and DEX platforms are observability overlays.

    Each device receives a composite DEX score (0–100) calculated as a weighted blend of device health (30%), application responsiveness (40%), and employee-reported sentiment (30%). Devices below 60 are flagged as poor experience and surface in the productivity-impact card — with an estimate of hours lost per user per quarter.

    Underlying data — unified telemetry layer

    Every device record on this screen carries a unified field set assembled from three telemetry sources and reconciled against Active Directory as the master identity:

    • Active Directory (master) — adObjectGuid, adUserPrincipalName, adDistinguishedName, adLastLogon. Used as the join key.
    • Intune (Windows enrolment & policy) — intuneDeviceId, complianceState, configurationProfiles, autopilotProfile, lastSyncDateTime.
    • Jamf Pro (Apple enrolment & policy) — jamfId, jamfSmartGroups, depProfile, filevaultStatus, lastInventoryUpdate.
    • Windows Performance Analyzer (DEX & telemetry) — bootDurationSec, logonDurationSec, cpuPressure, ramPressure, diskPressure, networkLatencyMs, packetLossPct, appCrashesPerWeek, collabCallQuality, sentimentScore, dexScore.
    • Business applications — appPerf object keyed by app name (M365, ERP, industry-specific). Each entry captures responseSec, crashesPerWeek, healthScore.
    How the WPA telemetry is collected

    Data is sourced from the Windows Performance Analyzer (WPA) together with the Windows Performance Recorder (WPR), which reads Event Tracing for Windows (ETW) — the kernel-level telemetry bus that has been built into Windows since Vista.

    ETW emits structured events for boot, logon, processes, threads, CPU sampling, file and disk I/O, network, memory, power, GPU, and most Windows subsystems. WPR captures and serialises those events into an .etl trace; WPA renders that data into graphs and tables, which are then aggregated by this platform into the per-device DEX fields above.

    ETW is a first-class Windows facility — no agent install, no driver. macOS and iOS contribute parallel telemetry via Apple's Endpoint Security framework and CoreAnalytics, surfaced into the same canonical schema.

    Per-tenant industry application set: Oil & Gas tenants surface SCADA HMI, well-data, and reservoir-modelling apps; Banking tenants surface trading platforms, payments-rail consoles, and risk & AML tooling; Transportation tenants surface dispatch, fleet-telematics, and crew-scheduling apps. Microsoft 365 and the four major ERP suites (SAP, Oracle Fusion, Workday, Salesforce) are present in every tenant.
